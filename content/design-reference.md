# Design Reference

This file is a shared visual reference for humans and AI tools. It describes how recurring UI patterns should feel and behave across the site.

It is guidance, not a rigid schema.

## Brand Feel

Edge8 should feel modern, credible, strategic, and execution-focused.

The visual tone should be premium and clear, not playful, noisy, or trend-chasing.

## Core Principles

- Keep layouts clean and easy to scan.
- Prioritize clarity over decoration.
- Use contrast to emphasize trust and action.
- Let headlines, proof, and CTAs carry the page.
- Keep visual rhythm consistent across sections.

## Color Direction

Use deep navy or similarly dark professional tones for authority.

Use mint or a similarly bright accent color for primary actions and emphasis.

Use white and soft gray backgrounds to create section contrast and page rhythm.

Avoid overly saturated palettes or decorative gradients unless a page has a clear reason for them.

## Typography Direction

Headlines should feel confident and concise.

Body copy should be easy to read and not overly dense.

Short support text can be used above or below major headings to create hierarchy.

## Spacing Direction

Use generous vertical spacing between sections.

Cards and content blocks should feel breathable, not compressed.

CTA areas should have enough spacing to stand apart from surrounding content.

## Section Headers

Section headers should feel consistent across pages.

Typical structure:

- a clear heading
- optional supporting sentence below it
- centered alignment when the section is broad and promotional
- left alignment when the section is more editorial or explanatory

## Buttons

Primary CTA buttons should feel consistent across the site:

- strong contrast
- simple label
- visually prominent without looking aggressive
- rounded corners are acceptable if they match the current site language

Secondary buttons can be outlined or lighter-weight, but should still feel part of the same family.

## Cards

Testimonials, blog previews, results, and case study previews should feel related even if they are not identical.

Shared traits:

- clear hierarchy
- enough padding
- readable titles and supporting copy
- restrained use of borders, shadows, or fills

Cards should not look overly glossy or startup-gimmicky.

## Testimonials

Testimonials should prioritize readability and trust.

Recommended traits:

- quote-first hierarchy
- author information below the quote
- simple card shell
- no decorative clutter that competes with the testimonial itself

If used in a row or grid, maintain equal visual weight across cards.

## Blog Cards

Blog cards should feel editorial and credible.

Recommended traits:

- image first when imagery exists
- category and date grouped together
- clear headline
- short excerpt
- obvious next action such as Read More

## Case Study Cards

Case study previews should feel proof-driven and slightly more outcome-oriented than blog cards.

Recommended traits:

- strong project or company label
- concise category or tag
- enough visual distinction to feel like portfolio evidence

## Results and Proof Sections

Metrics, outcomes, and proof points should feel structured and easy to scan.

Use icons only if they help orientation. Do not rely on them to carry the meaning.

## Hero Sections

Hero sections should establish confidence quickly.

Recommended traits:

- one strong headline
- one supporting line
- one primary CTA
- media only when it supports the message instead of distracting from it

If a hero uses video or imagery, text readability must remain strong.

## Contact and Conversion Sections

Final CTA and contact sections should feel calm, clear, and decisive.

Avoid over-designing these sections. The action should remain obvious.

## Responsive Guidance

On mobile:

- stack cards vertically when needed
- preserve headline clarity
- keep CTA buttons easy to tap
- avoid overcrowded multi-column layouts

On desktop:

- use spacing and grouping to create rhythm
- keep scan paths obvious
- avoid overly wide text blocks

## Allowed Variation

Pages do not need to look identical.

Variation is fine when:

- the page goal is different
- the content type changes
- the hierarchy needs to shift

Consistency should come from shared tone, spacing discipline, CTA treatment, and card/header patterns.
